Class Division, Poverty, and Brisbane

A COMU3100 Digital Research Proposal

Concept

In Australia, class is not very visible. We’re a have-a-go country where the concept of the blue-collar hard worker is idolised.

We don’t have the slums or extreme poverty that other countries have - our governments and councils do their best to keep Australia’s domestic landscape looking neat and proper.

But don’t be fooled. The divisions of class are hidden, yet if you look hard enough you will find very tangible class structures. And these structures confine us in all sorts of ways. Especially those of us who struggle to maintain a stable living environment.

Visual References

Breadline Kids, a documentary series by the BBC, shows an intimate, domestic window into the experiences of poverty in the UK.

The camera is a gentle observer in the documentary's environments. Interactions with the subjects are social and friendly, placing the audience emotionally into the experiences of the individuals the film depicts.

ACOSS's video about inequality does a great job not only in visualising statistics about inequality in a compelling way, but also by framing them directly in terms of human experience.

By placing the statistic into a lived environment, it directly links the emotional underpinnings of numbers.

Firestorm uses multimedia video as an atmospheric tool to convey information about the environment.

They create single snapshots in time to evoke an emotional response.

Similarly, Useful Gifts Uses embedded videos to engage an experiential side of its narrative.

It uses imagery which engages the senses, focusing on the tactility of its environments, contrasiting the lush grass with the hay bail on the dirt, and the grain.

Firestorm uses maps to contextualise and locate the concepts and emotions it portrays.

This grounds the narrative in a factual space, it cements it into the world of the viewer.

Argument & Narrative

We wish to illustrate to the audience that, within the apparently classless Brisbane environment, there are actually strong divides between social and financial mobility. Furthermore, these divides are ingrained in the physical, social, and geographic spaces of Brisbane.

To illustrate this argument, we will dive into the outer appearances of spaces we live in, to seek out the experiences of those who suffer from poverty. These experiences will then be contextualised within a bigger picture - a statistical one, but also a geographic one.

With this illustration, we hope to transform the abstract conceptualisation of poverty and class into ones that directly apply to the lives of their audience. As they move about the city in their day to day lives, we hope they actively think about how Australia’s class structures influence the way they interact with their local environment.

Primarily, we wish to engage those who live in Brisbane, but the local approach that this project will take can be applied to any other city.

Method & Platform

In order to convey the importance of class on living standards, we aim to marry the personal aspects of disadvantage with the mechanisms and structures under which class operates.

In terms of platform, we want to achieve three things:

First, we want to immerse the viewer into the experiences of the lower classes. The best format for this is video - the individual is placed with the camera into their environment, and the viewer can see it from their perspective.

Secondly, we want to represent relevant statistics and concepts that inform our thinking. Users should be able to process and consider these at their own pace, and consider the ramifications for themselves.

Third, we want to relate these facts back into the concrete world in which the audience inhabits.

In order to do both, we will take up a multimedia approach. A website containing both video to immerse viewers into a lived experience, and to control the visual hierarchy, while letting viewers explore the page at their own pace.

These elements will merge seamlessly, like they do in Firestorm.

Collaborative Production

Our production is driven by two parts.

The first is an in-depth ethnographic exploration into the financially disadvantaged areas of Brisbane. We plan to work with the organisations and charities to gain first-hand experiences of this environment, and will capture it in film and photographs as we go.

The second is the exploratory development of our site. We expect our approach to the site will evolve over time, and we want to achieve a good level of technical polish without biting off more than we can chew.

As such, we will take an incremental, experimental approach to developing our site. Each week or fortnight, we will decide on a part of the site to prototype, to test a new approach, technique or method of conveying information.
